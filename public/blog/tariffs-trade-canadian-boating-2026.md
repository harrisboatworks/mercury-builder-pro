## How we got here

Trade between Canada and the U.S. got complicated starting in early 2025:

- Early 2025: U.S. announced new tariffs on certain Canadian imports, including a 25% duty on goods that did not qualify under CUSMA rules of origin.
- Spring 2025: Canada responded with retaliatory counter-tariffs on selected U.S. goods.
- Summer 2025: Some U.S. tariffs on non-CUSMA-compliant Canadian goods rose as high as 35%.
- September 2025: Canada removed most retaliatory tariffs on U.S. imports while maintaining measures on steel, aluminum, and certain automotive goods.
- Late 2025 into 2026: U.S. Trade Representative began formal consultations for the CUSMA joint review scheduled for July 2026.

Goods that qualify under CUSMA, including most recreational boats and marine engines meeting content and origin rules, have continued to cross the border without added tariff charges under those rules.

## Why the boating industry is more cross-border than most

The Canada-U.S. marine relationship is deeply intertwined:
- Canada imports close to $1.1 billion CAD in U.S.-made recreational boats and engines annually.
- Approximately half of all U.S. recreational boat exports go to Canada.
- Canada recreational boating industry supports roughly 80,500 jobs and generates an estimated $13.9 billion in annual economic impact.

When trade barriers rise, both sides feel it.

## How tariffs actually affect prices

Even when a finished boat or engine qualifies for duty-free treatment, trade friction can affect pricing:

**Input costs.** Tariffs on steel, aluminum, and other raw materials increase production costs.

**Components.** Electronics, wiring, hardware sourced globally. Tariffs on Chinese or European components can add cost.

**Currency.** Trade uncertainty can weaken the Canadian dollar relative to U.S. dollar.

**Compliance overhead.** More complex origin documentation adds administrative cost.

## Legend Boats: Canadian-built, but not entirely insulated

Legend Boats are built in Ontario. There is no import duty when a Legend is sold to a Canadian customer.

That said, no boat builder operates on a purely domestic supply chain. Electronics, stainless hardware, upholstery materials, wiring, chargers, many components come from U.S., European, or Asian manufacturers. When tariffs affect those inputs, the cost can trickle through.

## Parts and accessories: where you feel it first

Electronics, batteries, trolling motors, chargers, and much of the hardware category rely on imported components. Price changes often appear as modest across-the-board increases rather than a single obvious spike.

## The "buy Canadian" effect

Industry surveys tracked by NMMA Canada suggest:
- About 56% of Canadian consumers report buying more Canadian-made goods.
- Around 58% say they are actively avoiding certain U.S. products where domestic alternatives exist.

For our customers, that often means genuine interest in Legend boats. For engines, Mercury remains the choice for most horsepower ranges because there is not a Canadian-manufactured equivalent in the 90 to 400 HP range.

## The 2026 CUSMA review: what is at stake

Under CUSMA, the three member countries are required to conduct a formal joint review six years after the agreement came into force. That review is scheduled for July 2026.

- Recreational marine products currently benefit from CUSMA provisions allowing duty-free trade under defined origin rules.
- NMMA testimony at USTR hearings emphasized maintaining these provisions.
- A smooth renewal provides long-term certainty.
- A contentious renegotiation could introduce real tariff risk on boats and engines that currently move duty-free.

## FAQs

### Will tariffs make my new Mercury cost more?

Mercury outboards qualifying under CUSMA continue to enter Canada without added CUSMA-based tariffs at this time. There has not been a direct tariff surcharge on engines sold through Canadian dealers. Broader cost pressures on inputs could gradually affect pricing.

### Are Legend Boats protected from tariffs?

Legend hulls are built in Ontario, so there is no import duty on the hull itself. Components and materials in every build come from global sources, so Legend is not fully insulated from trade-related cost increases.

### What is CUSMA and why does it matter for boating?

CUSMA (Canada-United States-Mexico Agreement, successor to NAFTA) sets the rules for duty-free trade between Canada, U.S., and Mexico. Recreational boats and marine engines that meet its rules of origin cross the border without added tariffs.

### When is the CUSMA review scheduled?

The formal joint review is scheduled for July 2026.

### Should I rush to buy before the review?

There is a reasonable argument for completing planned major purchases before the July 2026 review removes certainty. That is not a prediction that prices will spike, it is about eliminating one variable on your own timeline.

### What parts are most likely to be affected by tariffs?

Electronics, batteries, trolling motors, and imported hardware are most exposed because they often rely on non-CUSMA-origin components.
