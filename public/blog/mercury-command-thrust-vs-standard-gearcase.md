## What Command Thrust actually changes

Command Thrust isn't a power upgrade. Same block, same fuel injection, same horsepower. What CT changes is how that horsepower reaches the water. Three things:

1. **Bigger gearcase.** The lower unit is physically larger, more metal, more internal volume, room for bigger gears.
2. **Lower gear ratio.** A 60 HP CT runs roughly 2.38:1 where a standard 60 HP runs roughly 2.07:1. The prop spins slower for any given engine RPM, which means each turn of the prop does more work.
3. **Bigger prop swing.** The CT gearcase fits a larger-diameter prop. More blade area in the water means more torque transferred per revolution.

Stack those three changes together and you get more torque at the prop at any given RPM. In the real world that translates to a stronger hole-shot, better authority pushing a heavy load, and a small reduction in top-end speed because the engine has to spin faster for the same boat speed.

This is a torque-versus-top-speed trade. It is not "Command Thrust is better." It's "Command Thrust is better for these jobs and worse for those jobs." Naming the trade honestly is the whole point of this guide.

## When Command Thrust is the right call

CT earns its premium when the boat is heavy, the load is real, or low-speed authority matters more than the last few mph at the top end:

- **Pontoons, especially triple-tube and bigger.** Pontoons are heavy and high-windage. Moving them needs torque, not RPM. CT is almost always the right pontoon answer in the 60–150 HP range.
- **Heavy-load fishing rigs.** Three anglers, full livewell, full fuel, trolling motor batteries, gear. CT helps the boat plane at lower speeds and stay on plane through chop instead of falling off and plowing.
- **Houseboats and cruisers in the 60–150 HP range.** Displacement and semi-displacement hulls benefit from the torque the way a loaded truck benefits from a low gear.
- **Sailboat auxiliary applications.** Docking authority in current and wind matters more than top speed when you're parking a 28-footer between two other hulls.
- **High-altitude lakes.** Rare in Ontario but real on some northern routes, thinner air costs the engine power, and CT helps the prop bite harder to compensate.

If your boat is in any of those buckets, the CT premium pays back the first time you load it heavy.

---

## When standard gearcase is the right call

Honest counter-positioning. Standard wins when the boat is light and the priority is top speed:

- **Light bass boats running for top speed.** Standard gearcase, well-matched prop, higher gear ratio, that combination wins on outright knots every time on a light tournament hull.
- **Skiffs and tinnies under 16 ft.** A light hull doesn't need the torque. CT is overkill and you give up top end for a benefit you'll never use.
- **Older Mercury rigs sized for standard.** If the existing rigging, transom cutout, and prop inventory are all set up for standard, swapping to CT may require new rigging on top of the gearcase cost.
- **Anglers who genuinely prioritize top end over hole-shot.** Pro XS standard often beats Pro XS CT for outright top speed on a light hull. If the win condition is mph, go standard.

There's no shame in standard. Most Mercury motors ship as standard for a reason, it's the right answer on most light hulls running normal loads.

## The Command Thrust HP lineup

Mercury offers Command Thrust across a meaningful range of the lineup, but availability shifts year to year and model to model. The pattern, not the spec sheet:

- **25–150 HP FourStroke:** CT widely available, especially on the most common pontoon HPs (60, 90, 115, 150).
- **Pro XS 175–200:** CT is offered and useful on heavily loaded performance fishing rigs.
- **Above 200 HP:** CT availability narrows. Big V8s rarely use it because the application is usually light, fast hulls.

## Real-world hole-shot comparison

Take a typical 24 ft pontoon, full cottage load, eight to ten adults, coolers, kids on tubes:

- **Standard 90 HP:** struggles to plane with 8+ adults. Roughly 20 seconds time-to-plane in good conditions, often refuses to plane at all if the wind is up.
- **Command Thrust 90 HP:** planes confidently with 10+ adults. Roughly 10–12 seconds time-to-plane.
- **Top speed difference:** small, usually 1–2 mph in favour of standard.
- **Hole-shot difference:** large, and it's what owners actually feel every time they leave the dock.

Approximate, of course. Actual numbers depend on the specific hull, the specific load, the specific prop, and conditions on the day. The point isn't the exact seconds, it's the size of the gap. CT changes how the boat behaves with weight in it, and that's what matters on a pontoon used for the job pontoons are used for.

## Prop selection on Command Thrust

CT is a torque story, and the prop is how that torque reaches the water. Get this wrong and you've spent the CT premium for no benefit.

The pattern: CT gearcases swing bigger-diameter props than standard. On a 60–90 HP CT you're typically running 14"–15" diameter, where the standard equivalent runs 11"–13". The Mercury Spitfire 4-blade is a popular pontoon CT prop because it bites and holds water well through chop.

Pitch is the variable. The right pitch depends on the hull, the typical load, and the cruise RPM you want to live at. Don't copy a pitch number off a forum. Get it matched to your actual rig. Wrong pitch on CT either bogs the engine below its torque band or revs past the rev limiter, and either way the CT premium is wasted. We sort prop on every CT job at delivery.

## CT and fuel economy

Mostly the same as standard at cruise. CT may burn slightly more fuel at wide-open throttle because the engine is spinning faster for the same boat speed. At realistic cruise RPMs the difference is minimal and not the reason to choose one over the other.

If you're hoping CT saves fuel, it doesn't. That's not its purpose. CT exists to move heavy boats. If fuel economy is the top priority, the lever to pull is HP sizing and cruise RPM tuning, not gearcase choice.

## Rigging considerations for a CT upgrade or repower

A few practical notes for anyone considering CT on a repower or a gearcase swap:

- **Transom weight.** A CT gearcase adds roughly 10–25 lbs over standard depending on HP. Most boats handle the added transom weight fine. On marginal applications it's worth noting.
- **Steering effort.** The bigger prop generates more steering load. Power steering takes care of it on bigger HPs; manual steering owners may notice a slight change in feel.
- **Engine height.** The bigger gearcase changes where the cavitation plate sits relative to the keel. Engine height on the transom may need adjusting to keep the plate at the right depth, too low and you lose top end, too high and the prop ventilates on hard turns.
- **Existing prop inventory.** If you're sitting on a quiver of standard-gearcase props, none of them fit a CT gearcase. Plan on a fresh prop selection.

None of this is hard. It just needs to be done right at install.

## FAQs

### What's the difference between Mercury Command Thrust and standard gearcase?

Command Thrust is a Mercury gearcase option with three changes vs standard: a physically larger lower unit, a lower (numerically higher) gear ratio, and a bigger propeller swing. Together those changes deliver more torque at the prop at any given engine RPM. The practical result is stronger hole-shot, better authority pushing a heavy or high-windage hull, and more confident low-speed manoeuvring. The trade is a small reduction in top end versus the same HP on a standard gearcase, because the engine spins faster for the same boat speed. CT isn't a power upgrade, same HP, same fuel injection, same block. It's a torque-vs-top-speed tuning of how the motor delivers power to the water.

### Is Command Thrust always better than standard?

No. CT is better when the boat is heavy, high-windage, or routinely loaded, pontoons, fishing rigs running three-up with full livewells, displacement and semi-displacement hulls. Standard is better when the boat is light and the priority is top speed, bass boats, small skiffs, tinnies under 16 ft. Standard gearcase paired with a higher gear ratio and a well-matched prop will outrun the same HP CT on a light hull every time. The right answer is genuinely application-specific. We've put CT on pontoons that needed it and standard on bass boats that needed top end, and the customers were right both times.

### Does Command Thrust slow down my top speed?

Slightly, yes. Because CT uses a lower gear ratio, the engine spins faster for the same boat speed, which limits how much speed you can squeeze out at wide-open throttle. On a typical pontoon application the difference is roughly 1–2 mph at the top end versus a comparable standard setup, small enough that most owners never notice. On a light bass boat tuned for outright speed, the gap widens and matters more. If you regularly run the boat flat-out and care about the last 3 mph, standard is the right call. If you mostly cruise and care about getting onto plane fast with a load, CT wins.

### Do I need Command Thrust on my pontoon?

Almost always, in the 60–150 HP range. Pontoons are heavy and high-windage, moving them needs torque, not RPM. CT planes confidently with a real load (8–10+ adults, coolers, tubes, gear) where the same HP on a standard gearcase struggles or refuses to plane at all. We rig CT on most pontoon orders by default unless the customer specifically asks for top end. The only common exception is a smaller two-tube pontoon with a light owner who never carries passengers, where standard can work fine. If you fish, tube, or carry a typical cottage load, go CT.

### Can I swap from standard to Command Thrust on my existing motor?

On many Mercury models, yes, the gearcase is a serviceable assembly and Mercury offers CT lower units for a range of HPs. The swap requires the matching CT gearcase, a properly sized CT-spec propeller, and usually some attention to engine height on the transom because the bigger gearcase changes the cavitation plate position relative to the keel. It's not a 30-minute job and it's not free, but it's a real option on a healthy motor. We see it most often on pontoon owners who repowered with standard and learned the boat needed CT. Bring the motor in and we'll tell you honestly whether the swap makes sense for your specific block and model year.

### What size prop should I run on Command Thrust?

CT swings a bigger-diameter prop than standard, typically 14"–15" diameter on a 60–90 HP CT versus 11"–13" on a comparable standard gearcase. The Mercury Spitfire 4-blade is a popular pontoon CT prop because it bites the water hard and holds it through chop. Pitch tuning depends on hull, load, and target cruise RPM, and the right pitch on one boat is the wrong pitch on another. Don't pull a pitch number off the internet, get the prop matched to the actual rig. Wrong-pitch CT will either bog the engine or scream past the rev limiter, and either way you've lost the benefit of going CT in the first place.

### Does Command Thrust cost more?

Yes, modestly. The CT gearcase and the CT-spec prop carry a price premium over the standard equivalents, exact difference depends on HP and current Mercury pricing. On a typical pontoon repower the CT premium is a small fraction of the total job and it pays back the first time the boat planes with a full load instead of plowing. For up-to-date CAD pricing on a CT vs standard configuration for your boat, run a quote at [mercuryrepower.ca](https://www.mercuryrepower.ca) or call HBW. We'll show both options side-by-side and let the numbers and the use case decide.

### How do I know if my Mercury already has Command Thrust?

Two ways. Visually, a CT lower unit is noticeably bigger and beefier than the standard gearcase on the same HP, you can see it next to a standard motor of the same size. By model designation, most Mercury 4-stroke CT models carry a "CT" suffix in the model code (for example, 90ELPT Command Thrust). The model decal on the cowl and the serial number plate both spell it out. If you're not sure, send us a photo of the cowl decal and the lower unit and we'll confirm in a minute. Knowing what's on the boat now matters when you're spec-ing a repower or a gearcase swap.
